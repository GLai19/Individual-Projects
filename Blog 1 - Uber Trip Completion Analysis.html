The Data¶

The data we use today are driver data requested on Kaggle for 266 drivers in the New York City area. Variables of this dataset include pickup point, driver id, completion status, ride request time, and ride drop off time. Each row of this dataset represents an uber ride. An initial glance of the dataset implies that there aren't many variables to work with. Logistic Regression¶

Determine Factors influencing Trip Completion¶

After we have done our preliminary data exploration, we have a pretty good idea what the data offer and what we can do to get insights. To model a dependent variable like status (Trip completed vs Trip not completed). We need to use Logistic Regression to model a binary variable.

To simply explain what Logistic Regression is, Logistic Regressions are used most frequently to find answers (dependent variable) that are "YES" or "NO", or that resulted in two outcomes (I am going to Boston VS I am going to New York). For instance, credit card companies frequently use logistic regressions to see if factors (independent variables) such as APR rate, credit score, income affect binary outcome credit default (again - default VS no default). What factors contributes to credit default and what factors don't.

For the purpose of our study, we are trying to see factors that contribute to whether driver completes a trip or not. Insights gathered from our logistic regression result can then further used to help Uber improve its routing algorithm or help driver optimize its pickup preformance.

To start off, we first create our binary dependent variable Trip Completion (True = Completed, False = Not Completed), as well as all the independent variables we are planning to use to see how it affects trip completion

After we have sorted our data, we are ready to reshape our dataset for Logistic regression and one of the most important things to do is to create a test-train dataset

Why is a test train dataset important?¶

A test train dataset is important because often times, the randomly selected sample we use for modeling is not representative of the population (there may be bias existing in the sample). Thus, the next best thing to do to validate our sample set is to test our model with another randomly selected sample to see how our model performs generally from sample to sample. In the future, we will also introduce a more robust validation technique called k-fold cross validation. But for now, a test-train split should suffice

From our regular logistic regression result here we can see that all variables are highly significant with a very low P value, suggesting that both request hour and location contribute quite significantly to whether a driver completes a trip or not.

In statistics, a low P value suggests that the result of a given variable (say City) is highly significant in influencing the dependent variable (Trip Completion)

Moreover, all the coefficients (coef on our result)here are negative, signalling 2 important insights from this particular dataset. Firstly, the earlier an uber is being requested, or in the morning, the more likely a trip is to be completed vs a trip requesting at night. Secondly City pick-ups seem to result in higher rate of completion (greater coefficients) than airport pickups. This is a logical conclusion since airport uber pickups are usually very tricky with current taxi laws. (Anyone who has ever had to call an Uber should know the pain!)

Summary and Takeaways¶

There are two main insights we have gained from this dataset and analysis

  1. Uber demands are always higher than supplies, especially during peak hours when demands skyrocketed, which explains surge pricing. Nonetheless, companies wishing to ease the supply and demand gaps should explore better routing for car pooling service
  1. From our logistic regression result, it looks like that Airport pickups (P-value = 0.033, coefficient = -0.1) are more likely to result in Trip cancellation and requests not met than city pickups. The reationale behind this could be that airport pickups tend to be more convoluted than city pick ups, with no designated pickup spots in many airports in New York City and riders having difficulties to locate their drivers. Currently, the pick up inbalance can be due to policies with the New York City Yellow cabs, but management wishing to improve airport pickups for uber can explore other options such as setting designated rideshare pickup spots as well as increase use of uber pool in airports
